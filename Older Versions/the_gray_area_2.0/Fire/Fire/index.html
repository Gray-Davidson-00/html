I am a member of two performance ensembles, Weapons of Mass Distraction, based out of Reed College in Portland Oregon is a highly skilled amateur troupe who meet weekly to share skills and perform. Weapons regularly produces 5-6 shows each year including a mythology based theatrical show each spring. I began training with Weapons in 2007 and have performed in nearly all of the troupe's shows since that time. I am still an active member of the group despite having since graduated from Reed.

My second troupe, The Original Black Rock City Garnish, is a more professional group comprised of performers of many backgrounds. Many members of thr group are performers in various dance or burlesque professions, others are teachers and professional performers of partner acrobalance, and many are fire performers. Each of us taches the others our individual art in order to form a stunning spectacle. Each year Garnish choreographs and practices a continuous 20-30 minute performance employing every fire prop we posess, and submits a video to the competition run by the Burning Man Fire Conclave. Our video has been accepted now for twelve years in a row, and the set is then performed on Saturday night of the Burning Man Festival in front of an audience of 60,000 people.

While choreographed shows and collaborations with other performers often leads in new and intersting directions, the majority of my performance and practice has been improvised and solo. I try to achieve a gracefulness in my performance through a focus on dance- and martial arts-influenced movement rather than through speed. I have studied blues dance, tango, capoeira, tai chi and acrobatics to improve my fire performance, and have attended workshops and informal practices with professionals from five continents.

As fire dancing is an artform that involves open flames, performers and lawmakers are extremely careful to ensure that no individual on or off the stage recieves injury as a result of the spectacle. Weapons of Mass Distraction employs extensive training sessions, which I have for three years been in charge of administering, before allowing new members to practice or perform with fire. The members of Garnish tend to join the group already seasoned fire performers, but in neither group do we ever perform without the proper safety equipment and personel at hand in case of accident.
